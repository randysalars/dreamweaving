# Why Cash Is Not Safety

**Direct answer:** Cash is a form of optionality, but it is not safety by itself. Safety depends on access, purchasing power, and resilience under stress.
**Mechanism:** Cash fails as safety because it can lose purchasing power, become inaccessible, or be insufficient against large shocks.
**Implication:** Treat cash as a tool. Build safety as a system: liquidity, redundancy, skills, and low dependency.

## Definitions

- **Cash:** the most liquid store of purchasing power.
- **Safety:** ability to survive shocks without irreversible loss.
- **Inflation:** erosion of purchasing power.
- **Access:** ability to use resources when needed (banking, controls, timing).

## The mechanism (why this works)

1. Cash provides flexibility for many common shocks.
2. But safety depends on the size and type of shock and your dependencies.
3. Therefore, cash is one layer of resilience, not the whole foundation.

## Where this breaks down

- In short crises, cash can be the best tool.
- In long inflationary periods, cash can quietly destroy purchasing power.
- If your true risk is dependency (one income stream), cash alone does not fix it.

## Practical use (evergreen)

If you understand this model, you should:

- Stop optimizing: **a large balance as a substitute for resilience**
- Start measuring: **runway, dependency count, and access risk**
- Redesign: **safety as layers: cash buffer, diversified income, skills, and low fixed obligations**
