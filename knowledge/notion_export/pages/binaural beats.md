<details><summary>Creating binaural beats </summary>
  to use as background sounds in a video requires generating specific sound frequencies, blending them properly, and then embedding them into your audio or video project. Below is a step-by-step guide on how to create binaural beats:

  ---

  ### Step 1: Understand Binaural Beats

  - What are they? Binaural beats occur when two slightly different frequencies are played in each ear, creating a third, perceived frequency (the "beat") by the brain.
    - Example: If 210 Hz is played in one ear and 200 Hz in the other, the brain processes a beat at 10 Hz (the difference).
  - Purpose: Binaural beats are used for relaxation, focus, meditation, and inducing altered states of consciousness by targeting specific brainwave frequencies:
    - Delta (0.5–4 Hz): Deep sleep and relaxation
    - Theta (4–8 Hz): Meditation, creativity, and relaxation
    - Alpha (8–14 Hz): Focus, calmness, and relaxation
    - Beta (14–30 Hz): Alertness and active thinking
  ---

  ### Step 2: Tools You’ll Need

  - Free Software Options:
    - Audacity: A free, open-source audio editor with the ability to generate tones.
    - Gnaural: Specifically designed for binaural beats generation.
    - Brainwave Generator (paid): A dedicated binaural beat generator.
  - Headphones: Ensure binaural beats work properly by using stereo headphones, as one frequency plays in each ear.
  ---

  ### Step 3: Generate the Base Frequencies

  - Open your chosen audio software (e.g., Audacity).
  - Generate two sine wave tones:
    - Select Generate > Tone.
    - Set a base frequency for one ear. Common frequencies for relaxation range from 200 Hz to 400 Hz.
    - Generate another tone with a slightly different frequency (e.g., 200 Hz in one ear and 210 Hz in the other).
  - Assign each tone to left and right channels:
    - Pan the 200 Hz tone to the left channel.
    - Pan the 210 Hz tone to the right channel.
  ---

  ### Step 4: Add the Binaural Beat Effect

  - The "beat" is the difference between the two frequencies. For relaxation or trance states, aim for:
    - Delta (0.5–4 Hz) for deep sleep or hypnosis.
    - Theta (4–8 Hz) for meditation or creativity.
  - Adjust the second tone so its frequency offsets the first by your desired brainwave frequency difference. For instance:
    - First tone = 200 Hz
    - Second tone = 204 Hz yields a 4 Hz Theta wave.
  ---

  ### Step 5: Blend with Background Music

  - Import or create light ambient music or white noise (e.g., soft waves, rain, or wind sounds).
  - Mix the binaural beats with the background track, ensuring:
    - Ambient sounds do not overpower the binaural frequencies.
    - Adjust the volume of the beats to a subtle level (low but perceptible).
  ---

  ### Step 6: Export and Test

  - Export the audio in high quality (e.g., WAV or MP3) to preserve tonal accuracy.
  - Test using stereo headphones to ensure the binaural effect works properly before finalizing the video.
  ---

  ### Step 7: Sync with Video

  - Use video editing software (e.g., Adobe Premiere Pro, Final Cut Pro, DaVinci Resolve, or Camtasia) to sync the binaural beats audio with your video.
  - Loop the binaural beats if needed to match the video length.
  ---

  ### Pro Tips

  1. Layering Techniques: Add multiple background layers for a richer soundscape (e.g., forest + river + soft binaural beat underneath).
  1. Fades and Transitions: Smoothly fade binaural beats in and out so they don't start or stop abruptly.
  1. Protect Volume Levels: Keep binaural beats and background music at a calming and consistent volume to avoid distracting changes.
  1. Target Audience: Decide which purpose you're optimizing for (e.g., hypnosis requires Theta or Delta, focus may require Alpha or Beta).
  1. Add Natural Environmental Sounds: Incorporating subtle environmental noises can make the beats feel immersive.
